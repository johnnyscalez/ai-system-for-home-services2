The 5 questions — engineered with intent
Questions That Engineer Conviction
Each question has ONE job. If you ask them in order, by question 5 the prospect is not evaluating your offer — they're announcing their own conclusion.
1
Pain Memory Access
"When's the last time you were on a job, hands full, and a new lead came in that you just couldn't get to? What happened to it?"
Forces them to access a specific memory — not give a generic answer. A specific memory activates the emotional response of that moment. Their body re-experiences the frustration. You are not creating the pain — you're making existing pain vivid. This is the difference between "I have a problem" and "I FEEL a problem."
→ If they can't recall: "Does that happen more in summer? Like right now?" (They will say yes.)
2
Invisible Loss Reveal
"How many times a week do you think that happens — and you don't even find out until later, or never at all?"
"You don't even find out" is the key phrase. It opens the category of invisible losses — the money they're losing that doesn't show up anywhere. Most owners know about the lead they missed. They have no concept of the 10 they never knew about. This question creates that gap in their mind.
→ Follow up: "What's an average job worth for you — just a rough estimate?" → Now you have the number. You don't calculate it yet. You just hold it.
3
Permission Bridge
"What if I could show you something that handles that exact scenario — every single time, midnight on a Tuesday — and it costs less than what you spent on Facebook ads this week? Would it be worth 5 minutes?"
This is NOT a feature pitch. It's a pre-commitment question. By answering "yes" — and they always do — they've just told their own brain "I'm going to say yes if it delivers on that." You've set the standard. The demo now just has to meet it. You've also anchored cost ("less than your weekly ad spend") before they even see a number.
4
Post-Demo — Future Pacing
"What would your business look like if every single lead that just filled out your form — right now, while we're on this call — got that exact response?"
Asked AFTER they've experienced the demo. They're still in the emotional high of seeing the system work. This question makes them project themselves into the future where the system is already running. They're not evaluating a purchase — they're imagining ownership. The brain treats vivid imagination as near-experience. You're installing a picture of success before the objection conversation starts.
→ Let them answer fully. Don't rush to the offer. This is worth 60 seconds of silence.
5
Calculator Moment — Their Number
"You said about [X] leads go cold a week. At [their ticket], that's — what does that add up to per month? I want you to say the number."
This is the most important question in the process. YOU know the answer. But when THEY calculate it and SAY it — it becomes their number, not yours. Information you give someone has 40% retention. Information they generate themselves has 90%+ retention and emotional weight. "I said it — therefore it's real." The decision is 80% made the moment they say their own loss number out loud.

The First Half
The first three moves have one job: get them to feel the cost of the status quo so viscerally that the demo feels like relief, not a sales pitch.
1
The Pain Access Open
2 minutes · No pitch · No demo · Just listen
Activate the feeling

Start here. Not with "let me tell you what I built." Not with features. Open by making them access a real painful memory. Their body will do the selling for you.

"Hey [Name] — really glad you jumped on. Before I show you anything, I want to ask you something that'll make the demo 10x more relevant. Can I?" [They say yes.] "When's the last time you were on a job, completely buried, and a new lead came in that you just couldn't get to in time? Do you remember what happened to it?"
Why this works: You're opening with curiosity and a specific memory request. Not a pitch. The prospect relaxes because you're asking, not selling. And their answer will almost always include the exact pain your system solves — in their own words, which you'll mirror back later.
Listen for their exact language. Write down what they say. "Dispatch chaos" · "slipped through" · "called back two hours later, they'd already booked someone else." These exact phrases go back into your pitch later and trigger the feeling again.

Natural follow-up (read the room, use one):

  • "How often does that happen — especially in a week like this one?" (It's peak summer. Right now.)
  • "It's [X]° in [their city] right now — I'm guessing your dispatch board looks pretty intense."
  • "And when you found out that lead went somewhere else — what did that job end up being worth?"
2
The Invisible Money Question
2 minutes · Get the number · Hold it
Make the loss real

Don't calculate for them. Guide them to calculate for themselves. The number they say out loud lands 3× harder than the number you tell them.

"Okay. So how many leads a week do you think go cold — ones you meant to call back but just couldn't get to in time? Rough number." [They answer. Let's say "3-4 a week."] "And your average job — what does a typical install or repair run? Ballpark is fine." [They say $4,000-$6,000.] "So [4 leads] × [$5,000] — what does that add up to per week?"
The math moment: Let them do it. "$20,000 a week?" When THEY say that number, it's theirs. You can now reference it for the rest of the call as "your $20,000." Not a stat. Their number.
After they say the number: pause 3 full seconds. Let it sit. Don't rush to "well that's why I built this." The silence gives the number room to land emotionally. This is the single most skipped technique in sales and the single most valuable one.
3
The Permission Bridge
30 seconds · Pre-commitment · No resistance
Install the yes before the demo

Before you show anything — get a conditional yes. This one sentence completely restructures the demo from "let me pitch you" to "I'm just confirming what you already said you wanted."

"What if I could show you something that handles that exact scenario — every lead, midnight on a Tuesday, when you're on a roof — and it costs less than what you spent on Facebook ads this week. Worth 5 minutes?"
Why this works: Cost is anchored to "what you spent on ads this week" — a number they're comfortable with. They've already said yes to the ROI before seeing the price. You haven't pitched a feature. You've described their outcome. By answering yes, they've committed to "if it does that, I'm in." The demo just needs to deliver.
Then say: "Okay. Grab your phone. I'm going to do something a little different."

Moves 5–7: Build Certainty, Then Close
5
Future Pace — Let Them See the Win State
90 seconds · One question · Let them answer fully
Install ownership

They've just experienced the system. Now you ask them to project themselves into the future where it's already running. This is the most underrated move in the process. Don't skip it.

"Quick question — and I want you to actually think about this for a second. What would your business look like if every lead that hit your Facebook form got that exact response? Every one. While you're on the roof. While you're at dinner with your family. What changes?"
Why this works: You're not asking "do you like it." You're asking them to describe their own future. The brain experiences vivid future projection as near-reality. They're not buying software — they're buying the version of their business they just imagined. Let them paint it. Don't rush them.
Listen for the words "I wouldn't have to" or "I could finally." These are the moments where they've crossed from evaluating to desiring. Once they say those words, the sale is 90% done. You just need to not mess it up.
6
The Math They Build — Their Number
2 minutes · Calculator · They type it
Certainty through math

You already got their lead count and ticket size in Move 2. Now you bring it back. Don't use a spreadsheet — use a visible calculator on screen and have them say the number.

"Remember you said about [X] leads go cold a week? At [$Y] a job — what does that come to per month? I want to hear you say it." [Pause. They calculate.] "That's the number this system is competing against. Not other software. Not $297. Your [their number]."
Then the anchor: "One extra booking from this system per month covers the cost for 20 months. This isn't about cost. It's about which world you want to live in."
Visual trick: Pull up the calculator on screen, visible to them. Type in their numbers, let them confirm. This makes the math feel collaborative and real — not a vendor pitch deck showing favorable numbers.
7
The No-Risk Close
2 minutes · Full-price anchor → pilot offer → link in chat
Remove all friction

Every step has been building to this. The only remaining job is to remove the last bit of risk so saying yes is the obvious move.

"Here's what I want to do. Normally this is a $2,250 install plus $597 a month. For you — I'm waiving the entire install, and giving you the first 30 days completely free. The only thing I need is $200 — not a fee, it gets applied to your first month. It's just so I know we're both serious.

You just texted that number and saw what your leads experience. You just built the math. The only question left is: do you want that running on your real leads or not?

I'm sending you the link right now."
Send the Stripe link immediately in the chat while you're both on the call. Don't say "I'll email it." Type it in the chat right now. Every minute between "yes" and "paid" is a minute for doubt to creep in.

3 Rules for Move 4 (The Moment)

Rule 1: Dashboard must be empty when call starts. Their card appearing as the ONLY card = maximum visual impact.

Rule 2: Don't explain what's happening while it's happening. Let the system perform without narration. Commentary breaks the experience.

Rule 3: After you say "I didn't touch anything" — 10 seconds of silence. Count them in your head. Do not fill the silence.
