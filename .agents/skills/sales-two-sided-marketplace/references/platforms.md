## Point-of-buyer-signup clerk-flip

**What it is:** capturing supply leads from the same in-person sales call that closes the buyer. When the clerk says "I don't mind cleaning the bathrooms," you flip the objection into a recruit.

**Best for:** any marketplace running a `/sales-field-sales` motion. Highest fit, lowest cost.

**Fee model:** free.

**Pros:** pre-qualified candidates (already do the work, know the environment, live nearby), high retention because the buyer relationship anchors them.

**Cons:** only scales with your demand-side activity — not a substitute for paid channels at scale.

**Operational details:** train the field rep script to ALWAYS ask the clerk if they'd want side work, capture name + phone + days available, follow up via SMS within 24 hours.

## Existing-worker referrals

**What it is:** pay your active workers a flat bounty ($50-200) for referring new workers who complete N shifts.

**Best for:** after you have 3-5 active supply workers. Highest retention channel.

**Fee model:** $50-200 flat per successful referral, paid after N completed shifts (typically 3-5).

**Pros:** referred workers stay longer; the referrer becomes a hiring signal.

**Cons:** doesn't kick in until you have a base of supply already.

**Operational details:** announce the bounty in your worker app / SMS group, pay quickly when triggered, publicize each payout.

## Indeed

**What it is:** dominant general job board with pay-per-application and sponsored options.

**Best for:** all verticals, mid-to-tight labor markets.

**Fee model:** pay-per-application (varies, often $0.50-$2/click or $5-$15/applicant) or flat sponsored posting. Free organic posts are deprioritized.

**Pros:** largest applicant pool; integrates with Workstream and other ATS tools for SMS-apply.

**Cons:** quality varies widely; needs an SMS-apply or one-click apply flow to convert applicants in tight labor markets.

---

## Craigslist

**What it is:** old-school classifieds. Still effective in tight blue-collar / immigrant-labor markets.

**Best for:** tight labor markets, immigrant labor pools, blue-collar verticals (cleaning, lawn, courier).

**Fee model:** $7-50 per posting depending on city.

**Pros:** cheap; reaches workers not on app-based platforms; high response volume in some metros.

**Cons:** application quality is rough; lots of spam; no native ATS.

---

## ZipRecruiter

**What it is:** aggregator that distributes one post to 100+ boards.

**Best for:** all verticals, especially hospitality + restaurant.

**Fee model:** flat monthly or pay-per-click; mid-market pricing.

**Pros:** broader distribution than Indeed alone; mobile-optimized apply.

**Cons:** more expensive than Craigslist; cheaper than Workstream for pure posting.

---

## Workstream

**What it is:** all-in-one hiring + onboarding + payroll + scheduling for hourly multi-location teams. SMS-apply, Voice/Video AI screening, mobile onboarding. Used by 46 of the top 50 restaurant brands.

**Best for:** hospitality / restaurant / multi-location hourly hiring at scale.

**Fee model:** subscription, quote-based.

**Pros:** kills tool sprawl (hire + onboard + pay + schedule in one), 2-minute support response, 96.4% customer satisfaction.

**Cons:** built for restaurant/hospitality scale — overkill for a single-corridor pilot.

---

## Wonolo

**What it is:** on-demand staffing platform — companies post jobs, contractors or W-2 workers accept.

**Best for:** surge capacity, not core supply. Use as a fill-in layer when your own supply is at capacity.

**Fee model:** 45% markup on worker pay (verify with current rates).

**Pros:** instant fill, broad worker pool across industries.

**Cons:** expensive vs direct hire; workers are on Wonolo's platform, not yours — no loyalty.

---

## Instawork

**What it is:** on-demand staffing for hospitality + light industrial. Mix of W-2 and 1099 workers.

**Best for:** day-of-shift fill for hospitality + light industrial.

**Fee model:** variable markup based on amount offered to worker, demand, type of work, location.

**Pros:** mobile-first booking, fast fill, vetted worker pool.

**Cons:** less control than direct hire; workers can take competing shifts.

---

## Bluecrew

**What it is:** on-demand staffing for industrial / warehouse / light manufacturing. W-2 only.

**Best for:** industrial verticals where worker classification matters.

**Fee model:** ~40% markup on worker pay.

**Pros:** workers are W-2 employees of Bluecrew, sidestepping 1099 classification risk.

**Cons:** narrower vertical fit than Wonolo / Instawork; markup is high.

---

## Steady

**What it is:** worker-facing gig discovery app. Workers find side hustles; employers post gigs to reach this audience.

**Best for:** reaching side-hustler workers who use the Steady app to find supplemental work.

**Fee model:** variable; posting fees plus per-application costs in some categories.

**Pros:** reaches a specific demographic of part-time / side-hustle workers.

**Cons:** smaller employer-side presence; less polished than Instawork / Wonolo.

---

## Jobble

**What it is:** event staffing and side-hustle gig platform.

**Best for:** event staffing, brand activations, side-hustler audience.

**Fee model:** lower-friction posting; pay-per-engagement.

**Pros:** flexible postings, smaller minimum commitment than Instawork.

**Cons:** event-oriented audience may not translate to recurring service marketplace supply.

---

## Snagajob

**What it is:** large hourly hiring marketplace for retail + restaurant + hospitality.

**Best for:** hourly workers in retail / restaurant / hospitality verticals.

**Fee model:** subscription or pay-per-click; quote-based.

**Pros:** big hourly worker audience; vertical-fit for service marketplaces in restaurant / retail-adjacent space.

**Cons:** less focused on gig / 1099 than Instawork / Wonolo.

---

## Veryable

**What it is:** on-demand light industrial / warehouse labor.

**Best for:** narrow verticals — light industrial, warehouse, manufacturing.

**Fee model:** quote-based markup.

**Pros:** workers vetted for industrial settings.

**Cons:** wrong fit for cleaning / lawn / pet / home services.

---

## Gigpro

**What it is:** restaurant-specific gig staffing platform.

**Best for:** restaurant verticals — line cooks, dishwashers, servers.

**Fee model:** markup on worker pay.

**Pros:** vertical-specific; workers are pre-qualified for restaurant work.

**Cons:** restaurant-only.

---

## Sharetribe

**What it is:** no-code marketplace-builder platform (not a supply-recruiting channel, but worth noting for early-stage marketplace founders building the platform itself).

**Best for:** building the marketplace's transaction layer (listings, payments, messaging) without engineering.

**Fee model:** subscription, ~$99-299/mo.

**Pros:** ships a transactional marketplace in days; handles payments and messaging.

**Cons:** not a recruiting channel — included here as a marketplace-builder reference, not for supply acquisition.

## Channel selection grid

| Situation | Recommended primary channel |
|---|---|
| Running a field-sales motion already | Point-of-buyer-signup clerk-flip (always) |
| 3+ active workers, want retention | Existing-worker referrals + clerk-flip |
| Tight labor market, blue-collar vertical | Craigslist + Indeed |
| Restaurant / hospitality, scaling fast | Workstream (hire + onboard + pay in one) |
| Need surge capacity beyond own supply | Wonolo or Instawork (as fill-in layer) |
| Industrial / warehouse, W-2 needed | Bluecrew |
| Restaurant-only gig fill | Gigpro |
| Building the platform itself (transactional layer) | Sharetribe |
